\section{Introduction}

\citet{lusardiMitchell2014} and a large literature document that financially literate households make better portfolio decisions: they diversify more, panic less, time the market less, and pay more attention to fees and volatility-adjusted risk. The variation across U.S. states is substantial. Roughly 28 percent of adults answer three of five basic literacy questions correctly in high-literacy states like Minnesota and New Hampshire, against around 21 percent in Mississippi and West Virginia. The local-bias literature supplies the second piece: retail investors hold portfolios tilted toward locally headquartered firms \citep{covalMoskowitz1999, ivkovicWeisbenner2005}, and this local demand moves prices \citep{hongKubikStein2008, bernileKumarSulaeman2015}. Together they yield a clean hypothesis. Where retail investors are less literate, they should trade more like the textbook noise trader, so state literacy should moderate the momentum-IV interaction, a retail-friendly anomaly paired with the standard limits-to-arbitrage measure, with the effect weaker in literate states.

In aggregate, the data do not deliver. Regressing the three-way interaction on the full panel of CRSP-listed common stock returns a modest negative coefficient with marginal significance, and the magnitude all but vanishes once headquarters-state assignment is corrected for the 14 percent of firm-months whose static Compustat snapshot does not match the then-current 10-K filing. But the aggregate test pools firms with very different ownership structures, and it pools firms whose local-investor base is well-defined (stable headquarters) with firms whose base is in transition (relocators). So the aggregate null says little about whether a literacy channel exists. It says only that the channel does not operate uniformly across the cross-section.

The contribution of this paper is to ask a sharper question: where in the cross-section does the literacy mechanism become visible, under what conditions on firm ownership and size, and what does the answer say about the microfoundation? On the stable-headquarters subsample of CRSP common stock from 2009 to 2023, the literacy moderation concentrates in firms whose institutional ownership sits in the middle of the within-stock IO distribution, the mid-IO tercile, with mean ownership share around fifty percent. Firms with predominantly retail ownership (low IO) show essentially no moderation, and firms with predominantly institutional ownership (high IO) show none either. The mid-tercile coefficient survives once firm size enters as a competing moderator, so the finding is not a size effect relabeled as ownership, even though mid-IO firms are predominantly mid-cap. Within mid-IO, the coefficient maps to a cross-state literacy gradient in the predicted direction: higher-literacy states show weaker anomaly slopes, and the across-state correlation between per-state coefficients and state literacy is clear.

The interpretation the data point to is an \emph{interaction zone}. The literacy channel needs two ingredients to leave a price footprint: retail demand large enough to move prices, and institutional activity sufficient to translate that demand into return differences detectable across states. Where neither is present (micro-cap firms held mostly by retail), retail mispricing is real but accumulates uniformly and does not load on cross-state literacy variation. Where institutions dominate (large firms with high IO), arbitrage absorbs retail demand and neutralizes the local-state channel. The interaction zone is the set of firms with mixed ownership at meaningful market size, where both ingredients are present. In the full stable-HQ panel it falls predominantly in mid-IO; dropping the bottom 50 percent of firms by market capitalization shifts the moderation to low-IO, and the cross-state gradient shifts with it. It is the same economic population, relocated to a different tercile of the conditional IO distribution.

This migration is the paper's main piece of mechanism evidence, the signature an interaction-zone reading predicts and the IO-tercile result alone does not deliver. The high-IO tercile shows no cross-state gradient under any sample restriction, the null the reading predicts. A decomposition by idiosyncratic-volatility decile within mid-IO shows none of the strengthening-at-high-IV pattern the textbook limits-to-arbitrage story would require, which points the mechanism toward size and ownership conditioning rather than arbitrage cost.

Two facts bound the result. The continuous-margin quadratic-IO U-shape clears five percent on a quadratic form only, not under log or kink alternatives, so the functional-form-free claim is the discrete-tercile contrast. And under point-in-time literacy reassignment on the full panel the mid-IO coefficient shrinks sharply, so the characterization is a statement about firms with stable headquarters rather than the corrected full panel. An instrument built from state K-12 personal-finance graduation requirements is not available here, because mandate states are systematically less literate (states adopted mandates in response to low adult literacy); the OLS estimate is the preferred inferential statement, and the migration supplies the predicted-signature confirmation an instrument would otherwise provide.

The closest prior work is \citet{korniotisKumar2013}, who show that state-level business cycles predict returns of locally headquartered firms. This paper differs on three margins: the state variable is NFCS literacy rather than the business cycle, the object is a three-way moderation of an anomaly interaction rather than a level prediction, and the discriminating margin is a within-stock IO contrast the business-cycle channel does not predict. A business-cycle control block attenuates the literacy three-way by 20 to 23 percent, so the two state moderators are distinct but partly overlapping. \citet{korniotisKumarPage2020} use brokerage-demographic sophistication proxies but neither NFCS literacy nor the momentum-IV interaction, and \citet{hillertJacobsMuller2014} is the closest precedent for a state-level moderator of momentum, using media coverage with state individualism as the cross-sectional moderator rather than literacy.
