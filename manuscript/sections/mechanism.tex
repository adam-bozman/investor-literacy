\section{Mechanism: The Interaction-Zone Characterization}
\label{sec:mechanism}

Section~\ref{sec:results} documented four facts: the moderation concentrates in mid-IO ($\hat\gamma_{\text{mid}} = -0.0256$, $t = -2.24$); it relocates to low-IO when the bottom 50 percent of firms by market cap are dropped ($\hat\gamma_{\text{IO1}} = -0.0441$, $t = -2.04$); the per-state coefficient tracks state literacy in the cell that carries the moderation ($r = -0.28$ full-panel mid-IO, $r = -0.29$ above-median IO1) but not in high-IO ($r = -0.07$); and a decomposition by idiosyncratic-volatility decile within mid-IO shows no concentration at high IV. This section develops the interaction-zone characterization those facts identify and notes what the design does and does not separate.

\subsection{The interaction-zone reading}
\label{sec:zone}

The moderation lives where two conditions hold at once: retail demand large enough to move prices, and institutional activity sufficient to translate that demand into cross-state return differences. Where neither holds (micro-caps held mostly by retail), mispricing accumulates but does not load on cross-state literacy variation; where institutions dominate (large high-IO firms), arbitrage absorbs retail demand and the marginal investor is not a local trader. The interaction zone is the set of mid-cap firms with mixed ownership, where both conditions hold. Its IO label is sample-conditional: under the full-panel size composition it falls in mid-IO, because mid-IO firms are predominantly mid-cap, and under the above-median composition it migrates to low-IO, because low IO among large firms means retail-tilted at substantial size. Table~\ref{tab:migration} traces this cell by cell, and the high-IO null under both compositions is the null the reading predicts.

\subsection{Not a limits-to-arbitrage effect}
\label{sec:mech_iv_decile}

The textbook limits-to-arbitrage microfoundation \citep{shleiferVishny1997, pontiff2006} predicts that literacy-modulated retail demand should displace more mispricing where arbitrage is costlier, that is, at higher idiosyncratic volatility. A within-month NYSE IV-decile decomposition within mid-IO does not show this: the highest-IV decile is insignificant ($\hat\gamma = -0.035$, $t = -1.27$) and a joint equality test across deciles does not reject ($\chi^2(9)$, $p = 0.94$), with the full table in IA~\ref{ia:ivwithinmidio}. The channel we identify therefore operates through size and ownership conditioning, pinned down by the migration of Section~\ref{sec:migration}, rather than through arbitrage cost along the IV margin.

\subsection{High-IO null and college horse-race}
\label{sec:mech_hi_io}

The high-IO cross-state null ($\hat\beta_{\text{IO3}} = +0.018$, $t = +1.22$, $r = -0.07$; Holm $p_{\text{FWER}} = 0.996$) is the second pattern the reading anticipates: high-IO firms are held by nationally diversified institutions indifferent to headquarters state, so state-level literacy variation says nothing about the marginal investor, and whatever retail demand exists is arbitraged away. Two diagnostics rule out the obvious alternatives. Literacy is not a proxy for education: a state college-attainment horse-race within mid-IO leaves literacy sharp ($\hat\gamma_{\text{lit}} = -0.0350$, $t = -2.97$) and college null ($+0.00076$, $t = +0.65$) despite a $\rho = 0.62$ state-level correlation. And the mid-IO concentration is not a variance artifact, since mid-IO has lower identifying variation than low-IO yet a larger slope (Section~\ref{sec:robustness}).

\subsection{What the design does not separate}
\label{sec:mech_what_not}

The characterization is identified through the size-restriction migration and the cross-state gradient, but it does not separate three readings that share the same size and ownership conditioning: literacy-modulated retail demand \emph{intensity} (the reading we most naturally adopt, consistent with the gradient direction), literacy-modulated retail \emph{attention} (less momentum-following pressure in high-literacy states), and literacy-modulated retail \emph{timing} (a clientele-timing channel generating the same gradient). The three make different predictions for retail trade-flow data (volume, turnover, cross-spectral signatures) not available in the current panel; the TAQ-based Boehmer-Jones-Zhang retail trade indicator would separate them, a natural next-paper extension.

\subsection{Population scope}
\label{sec:mech_population}

The characterization holds on the stable-HQ subsample, on which the local-investor unit of analysis is held fixed. Under the point-in-time 10-K-header reassignment the mid-IO coefficient shrinks sharply (Section~\ref{sec:robustness}): the directional within-tercile pattern survives but the magnitude does not, so the characterization is a statement about stable-headquarters firms rather than the corrected full panel.
